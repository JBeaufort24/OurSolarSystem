Overview

Jupiter, the fifth planet from the Sun, is a world of extremes. As the largest planet in our solar system—more than twice as massive as all the other planets combined—it functions almost like a miniature solar system itself.

Jupiter is essentially the "Big Brother" of our solar system—it's protective, massive, and has a very busy social life (if you count 95+ moons as a social life).

Physical Characteristics

Jupiter is a gas giant, meaning it lacks a solid surface. If you attempted to stand on the planet, you would simply sink through increasingly dense layers of gas until the immense pressure crushed you.

  • Composition: Primarily hydrogen (75%) and helium (24%), similar to the composition of a star.
  • Size: Its radius is about 11 times that of Earth. You could fit approximately 1,300 Earths inside its volume.
  • Magnetic Field: Jupiter possesses a magnetic field 14 times stronger than Earth’s, creating intense radiation belts that can damage unshielded spacecraft.

    • 2026 Size and Shape Update

    Pecent findings from NASA’s Juno spacecraft (announced in February 2026) have refined our understanding of Jupiter's dimensions. Scientists found that Jupiter is slightly smaller and more "squashed" (oblate) than measurements from the 1970s suggested.

  • Equatorial Radius: Approximately 71,488 km (about 4 km smaller than previous estimates).
  • Polar Radius: Approximately 66,842 km (about 12 km smaller than previous estimates).
  • Oblateness: Its equator is about 7% larger than its polar diameter due to its incredibly fast rotation (one day is less than 10 hours).

    • Internal Composition and "Fuzzy" Core

    Jupiter lacks a solid surface. If you were to descend into the planet, you would pass through increasingly dense layers of gas and liquid.

  • Atmosphere: Primarily hydrogen (75\%) and helium (24\%), with traces of methane, ammonia, and water ice.
  • Metallic Hydrogen: At extreme depths, pressure turns hydrogen into a liquid metal that conducts electricity, generating Jupiter’s massive magnetic field.
  • The "Dilute" Core: Contrary to older models of a solid rocky core, Juno data suggests a "fuzzy" or dilute core. This means the heavy elements (rock and ice) are partially dissolved and mixed with the surrounding liquid hydrogen rather than forming a distinct solid ball.

    • The Great Red Spot and Weather

    Jupiter's atmosphere is a chaotic system of high-velocity winds and centuries-old storms.

  • The Great Red Spot: An anticyclonic storm larger than Earth. Recent studies indicate it is shrinking in width but may be growing taller.
  • Belts and Zones: The dark stripes (belts) are areas where gas is sinking, while the light stripes (zones) are where gas is rising.
  • Deep Winds: Juno has revealed that Jupiter's powerful atmospheric winds extend as deep as 3,000 kilometers below the cloud tops.

    • Details

  • 1. It’s a "Failed Star" (Sort Of) Jupiter has almost the exact same ingredients as the Sun—mostly hydrogen and helium. If it had been about 80 times more massive during its formation, it could have triggered nuclear fusion and become a star itself. Instead, it settled for being the largest planet.
  • 2. The Shortest Day in the Solar System Despite its massive size, Jupiter is a speed demon. It rotates so fast that a single day lasts only 9 hours and 56 minutes. This rapid spinning actually causes the planet to bulge at its equator, making it look slightly "squashed."
  • 3. It’s the Solar System’s Vacuum Cleaner Because of its immense gravity, Jupiter acts as a cosmic shield. It sucks in or deflects many of the comets and asteroids that might otherwise head toward the inner solar system (including Earth). We owe a lot of our planetary safety to Jupiter’s gravitational muscle.

    • Moons

    Jupiter is often called a "miniature solar system" because of its massive collection of moons. As of March 2026, there are 101 known moons orbiting the gas giant, though 95 of these are officially recognized by the International Astronomical Union (IAU).

    The system is dominated by the four Galilean moons, which are so large they can be seen from Earth with a good pair of binoculars.

    The Galilean Moons

    Discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610, these four moons account for 99.997% of the mass orbiting Jupiter.

  • 1. Io: The Volcanic World Io is the most geologically active body in the solar system, with hundreds of active volcanoes. Its vibrant yellow, red, and black surface is caused by constant sulfur eruptions. The intense gravity of Jupiter and the other moons "squeezes" Io, creating internal heat through tidal friction.
  • 2. Europa: The Ocean World Europa is one of the most exciting places in the search for life. It is covered in a thick layer of water ice, but evidence suggests a vast saltwater ocean lies beneath
    Recent Discovery (Jan 2026): Re-analysis of older data has confirmed the presence of ammonia-bearing compounds near fractures in the ice, a key building block for life.
  • 3. Ganymede: The Giant Ganymede is the largest moon in the solar system—it is actually larger than the planet Mercury. It is the only moon known to have its own magnetic field, which interacts with Jupiter's massive magnetosphere to create glowing auroras.
  • 4. Callisto: The Ancient Callisto is the most heavily cratered object in the solar system. Its surface is essentially a "time capsule" of the early solar system’s history, as it has remained geologically dead for billions of years.

    • Recent Moon Discoveries (2024–2026)

    In the last few years, our "census" of the Jovian system has grown significantly.

  • Small Irregular Moons: Most of the recently discovered moons (including the jump to over 100 in early 2026) are very small—only a few kilometers wide.
  • Origins: Many of these are believed to be captured asteroids or fragments of larger moons that were shattered during ancient collisions.

    • Active Missions to the Moons

    Humanity is currently sending two major "scouts" to investigate these worlds:

  • JUICE (JUpiter ICy moons Explorer): Launched by the ESA in 2023. It recently completed a flyby of Venus (August 2025) and is scheduled for a second Earth flyby in September 2026. It will arrive at Jupiter in 2031 to study Ganymede, Callisto, and Europa.
  • Europa Clipper: Launched by NASA in October 2024. This mission is focused entirely on Europa, aiming to determine if its subsurface ocean could truly support life. It is expected to arrive in 2030.
