Overview

Mars, often called the "Red Planet", is the fourth planet from the Sun and the 2nd smallest planet in the solar system. Its distinct reddish hue comes from iron oxide (rust) on its surface.

Mars is a cold, desert world with a thin atmosphere, located approximately 142 million miles from the Sun. It is one of the most explored bodies in our solar system and the only planet where we have sent rovers to roam the surface.

  • Light takes 8min and 20sec to travel from the Sun to your eyes
  • The "Atmosphere" is hotter than the "Surface"
  • The Sun is actually white

Feautures

    Physical Profile

    Mars is often called the "Red Planet" because iron minerals in the Martian soil oxidize, or rust, causing the soil and atmosphere to look red.

  • Size: With a radius of 2,106 miles, Mars is about half the size of Earth.
  • Gravity: Gravity on Mars is only 38% of Earth's. If you weigh 100 lbs on Earth, you would weigh only 38 lbs on Mars.
  • Atmosphere: The air is very thin and composed of 95.3% Carbon Dioxide, 2.7% Nitrogen, and 1.6% Argon. It lacks a protective magnetic field, leaving the surface exposed to cosmic radiation

    • Key Geographic Landmarks

    Mars boasts some of the most extreme geological features in the solar system, shaped by ancient volcanic activity and water flow.

  • 1. Olympus Mons The largest volcano in the solar system. It is a shield volcano that stands about 13.6 miles (22 km) high, making it nearly three times taller than Mount Everest.
  • 2. Valles Marineris A system of canyons that spans over 3,000 miles (4,800 km). If placed on Earth, it would stretch from New York City to Los Angeles. In some areas, it is 4 miles (7 km) deep.
  • 3. The Polar Caps Mars has permanent ice caps at both poles. During the Martian winter, carbon dioxide freezes out of the atmosphere as "dry ice" snow, covering the water-ice caps.

    • Time and Season

    Mars has a tilt on its axis (25 degrees) very similar to Earth's (23.5 degrees). This means Mars experiences seasons, though they last about twice as long because the Martian year is longer.

  • A Solar Day (Sol): 24 hours and 37 minutes.
  • Martian Year: 687 Earth days.

Details

    Mars is a world of extremes that feels both strangely familiar and completely alien. Here are some of the most fascinating "fun facts" about the Red Planet:

  • 1. Sunset in Blue
    On Earth, the sky is blue and sunsets are red. On Mars, it’s the exact opposite. Because the fine dust in the atmosphere scatters light differently, the daytime sky has a pinkish-red hue, while the sunsets appear blue to the human eye.
  • 2. Low Gravity Olympics
    If you stood on Mars, you could jump three times as high as you can on Earth. This is because Martian gravity is only about 38% of Earth's. A person who weighs 150 lbs (68 kg) on Earth would weigh only 57 lbs (26 kg) on Mars.

    Orbit and Rotation

  • Day Length - 24 hours, 37 minutes (slightly lomger than Earth
  • Year Length - 687 Days (Nearly twice as lomg)
  • Axial Tilt - 25.2°(very similar to 23.5°, causing seasons)
  • 3. The Grandest Canyon
    Mars has a canyon system called Valles Marineris that makes the Grand Canyon look like a tiny crack. It is:
    4 times deeper (up to 7 km or 4 miles deep).
    20 times wider.
    Long enough to stretch from New York City to Los Angeles.
  • 4. A Mountain That Touches Space
    The tallest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, is a "shield" volcano about the size of the state of Arizona. It is so tall (25 km or 16 miles) that its peak actually pokes out above the Martian atmosphere into space.
  • 5. Pieces of Mars on Earth
    We actually have physical pieces of Mars here on Earth. Scientists have found dozens of Martian meteorites—rocks blasted off the surface of Mars by massive asteroid impacts that eventually drifted through space and landed on our planet.

Moons

Mars has two small, irregularly shaped moons: Phobos and Deimos. Discovered in 1877 by astronomer Asaph Hall, these moons are much smaller than Earth’s Moon and are often described as looking like "lumpy potatoes" rather than perfect spheres.

The "Death" of a Moon

Mars has two moons, Phobos and Deimos. Phobos is orbiting so close to Mars that gravity is slowly pulling it inward. In about 50 million years, it will either crash into the planet or be ripped apart to create a rocky ring around Mars, similar to Saturn's rings.

    Phobos: The Doomed Moon Phobos is the larger of the two and orbits Mars so closely that it circles the planet three times a day.

  • The Stickney Crater: The most prominent feature on Phobos is a massive impact crater called Stickney, which is nearly 9 km wide. The impact was so powerful it nearly shattered the moon.
  • A Future Collision: Phobos is slowly spiraling inward toward Mars at a rate of about 1.8 meters every 100 years. Scientists predict that in 50 million years, it will either crash into the Martian surface or be ripped apart by gravity to form a ring of debris around the planet.

    • Deimos: The Smaller OutlierDeimos is roughly half the size of Phobos and orbits much further away.

  • Appearance: Unlike the cratered and grooved surface of Phobos, Deimos appears much smoother. This is because many of its craters have been filled in by a thick layer of loose rock and dust (regolith).
  • Escape Velocity: Deimos is so small and has such weak gravity that its escape velocity is only about 20 km/h (12 mph). Technically, a person riding a bicycle at full speed could launch themselves off the moon and into space.

    • Origin Theories
    There are two main theories regarding how Mars got its moons:

  • Captured Asteroids: Because their composition is similar to "C-type" asteroids found in the outer asteroid belt, many scientists believe Mars’ gravity snagged them as they passed by.
  • Impact Theory: A newer theory suggests that, like Earth’s Moon, they may have formed from debris kicked up after a giant object slammed into Mars early in its history.
